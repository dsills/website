Course Material

From the Catalog

An introduction to the fundamentals of environmental engineering and science such as chemistry, microbiology, mass balance, and reactor theory. Application of fundamental concepts to environmental engineering includes water quality, water and wastewater treatment, solid and hazardous waste, air pollution, greenhouse gases and climate change. This course includes hands-on laboratory component with a focus on experiential learning. Prerequisite: ENGR 222 or permission of the instructor.

Course Overview

In addition to covering fundamental principles (e.g., from chemistry, microbiology, and fluid mechanics) that govern environmental engineering, this course is also an opportunity for you to look critically at some of humanity's big problems. Here in the US, we face challenges associated with crumbling, out-of-date civil and environmental engineering infrastructure, and, in certain regions, looming problems of water scarcity. Around the world, 2.5 billion people lack access to basic sanitation (cite UNEP) and, according to WHO, 900 million people lack access to safe drinking water. And to make things even more interesting, climate change is expected to exacerbate many of the these problems.

Over the semester we will examine the current design of water and wastewater treatment systems, analyze these designs within the context of the principles of Green Engineering, and consider innovations for future designs that will provide solutions for regions without existing infrastructure. In addition to applying chemistry, microbiology, and physics to environmental problems, you will have a chance to engage in thoughtful intellectual conversations about current environmental issues both in writing and during in-class discussions. Learning how to talk enthusiastically and intelligently is an important skill you can learn in college, and you will have opportunityies to practice these skills in this class.

Course Goals

Note that ABET student outcomes will be assessed directly, as indicated by lower case letters.

  • Integrate and apply relevant content from previous courses---chemistry, fluid mechanics, calculus, and statistics---to identify, formulate, and solve environmental engineering problems. (a, e)
  • Apply the principles of material and energy balances to analyze environmental problems.
  • Understand the fundamental factors that affect the selection, design, and operation of pollution treatment technologies.
  • Identify appropriate technologies for treatment of specific pollutants present in water, soil, and air.
  • Design basic components of water and wastewater treatment reactors.
  • Analyze engineering designs through the principles of Green Engineering.
  • Develop an appreciation of the impact of civil and environmental engineering on society and the environment. (h)
  • Develop an appreciation for the need to assess and analyze environmental impacts of engineering designs.
  • Improve your ability to conduct experiments, analyze experimental data, and draw appropriate conclusions from experimental results. (b)
  • Improve your ability to communicate technical information in both written and oral form. (g)
  • Develop an appreciation for independent learning.

Labs

You will receive a syllabus for the lab part of the course on the first day of lab (Tuesday, 3 September), and information on labs will be distributed via Moodle. Labs will be held in the Environmental Lab, 366 Breakiron.

Exams and Quizzes

There will be two in class mid-term exams and one cumulative final exam. Make-up exams will only be available to students with written medical authorization from the Health Center or the Associate Dean of Engineering. Note that to pass this class your average exam grade must be at least 60%. In addition, there will be at least ten in-class quizzes, some of which will not be announced ahead of time. The goal of the quizzes is to help you keep up with reading and course work and alert me when important concepts are not clear. Quizzes will be held during the first 5–15 minutes of class (depending on how extensive they are) and there will be no make up quizzes. I will drop your quizz with the lowest score. Student athletes: please contact me if anticipate missing more than one class.

There will be ten problem sets over the semester that will give you the practice which is critical for succeeding on the exams. I encourage you to work with your classmates on the problem sets, but you must each submit your own work. In additon, please write the names of people you worked with at the top of each problem. No late homeworks will be accepted unless you made arrangements with me prior to the due date.

Participation and Professionalism

Attendance, while not mandatory, is highly recommended, and will contribute to the Participation and Professionalism part of your grade. In addition to listening to me lecture, you will actively work through problems during class, which should help you with homework, quizzes, and exams. Additionally, since I am trying to cultivate a learning community in the classroom, I ask that you arrive on time and prepared for class , and that includes completing reading assignments. I will call on students regularly, but only after I give you time (at least a couple of minutes) to think about a question I pose in class.

If you'd rather be sleeping, texting, facebooking, tweeting, shopping, or gaming, I suggest you do it somewhere more comfortable than the classroom. I prefer that you don't use laptops, tablets, or cell phones--unless it's helping you work on or solve an in-class problem.
